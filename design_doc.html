Abstract draft — three beats: problem, system, evidence

Shared physical workspaces are asynchronous by default: collaborators share machines and benches but rarely share the clock, and the judgments that matter — a swapped nozzle, a stale calibration, a fragile joint — die unspoken, because every messaging channel charges the person in flow a composition ceremony their observation cannot justify. We present Spatial Memory, a paradigm in which deliberate voice notes are captured at zero ceremony cost, anchored where they were spoken, and organized in a Memory Graph that keeps each place showing its current state rather than a pile of history. In two chained studies — leavers (N≈10) working hands-busy tasks, whose real utterances then seed the rooms that arrivers (N=12–16) walk into — zero-ceremony capture increased in-flow externalization over phone-and-channel practice, and graph structure outperformed raw anchoring on trap avoidance and triage under a week's accumulation.

1Introduction paper's job: make the tension visceral, kill the obvious alternatives (camera, channel), name the reframe — Moves 1–5

Premise. Physical space can now be made addressable: persistent shared anchoring, always-on head-worn capture, and on-device language models that transcribe and segment speech have converged. The instrumentation is first-person — worn by the collaborator, not fixed to the room.

Constraint. Shared physical work persists, and the people who share a room rarely share the clock. Labs, studios, shops, clinics: collaborators share equipment and benches but almost never overlap in time. The room is the only collaborator present across every shift — and it remembers nothing.

Problem. The room is the only witness, yet it is mute — and a camera cannot fix that, because the information that matters is not an observable event but a human's invisible judgment (a swapped nozzle, a stale calibration, a fragile joint). No camera, room-fixed or worn, can derive a recommendation from pixels. The judgment must be authored by the person who holds it — and that person is mid-task, hands on the work, rationally unwilling to pay the messaging ceremony.

The headline problem decomposes into four sub-problems — tagged A–D throughout this document. A, B, C are failures of the mute room; D is the failure Spatial Memory itself creates and must then solve — that two-level shape is what justifies the Memory Graph rather than plain anchoring.

Reframe. Keep the irreducible part (the human authors the content) and delete the removable part (the channel ceremony: naming a recipient, describing the referent, switching out of task). The person witnesses; the worn device silently captures what they chose to say; the room is the medium that carries it forward — anchored, structured, and navigable.

Why existing channels fail — the three gates

Every scenario beat and every study trap is built on information that fails Slack on at least two of three gates:

G1 · Recipient
Is the next reader genuinely unknown at compose time?

Channels demand a named addressee → broadcast spams, DM guesses, or nothing gets sent.

G2 · Referent
Does it point at a physical thing with an invisible property?

Lossy to describe in words, lossy to re-ground when read. "The 0.4 nozzle... on which printer?"

G3 · Moment
Is it useful only when standing at the object?

Phone delivery lands placeless and mistimed — before it matters, away from where it matters.

The camera gate (CAM). Even granting full room instrumentation: the valuable content is not an observable event but an authored judgment. A swapped nozzle, a stale-but-valid-looking calibration, a fragile joint — the scene looks identical either way. The judgment exists only in a person's head and must be spoken. This is the inversion of AsyncReality: their asymmetry is receiver-cannot-perceive (so room cameras suffice); ours is author-will-not-pay-the-ceremony (so the person must be the witness, and the worn device only removes the ceremony).

The strongest opponent is not the DM — it is the per-fixture channel. Real labs have #3dprinter and #mocap channels, and a channel does approximate G1: the channel is the machine's mailbox. What it cannot fix:

FailureWhy the channel loses
Delivery (G3)By the time the next person stands at the machine, the message is days deep in scrollback. Nobody re-reads a channel standing at the rig before pressing record.
Log vs. stateA channel is a time-ordered log; the room needs current state at a place. "Is the recalibration still pending, or did someone already do it?" is scrollback archaeology on Slack — and one supersedes edge in the Memory Graph. The next person sees the current answer, not the history.
CompositionStill requires phone-out, mid-pack-up, hands full of tripods.

We do not compete with Slack on its home turf: known-recipient, bounded, episode-class messages stay on Slack (§3) — the scenario shows this carve explicitly.

3Concept paper's job: define Spatial Memory as the user experiences it — properties, the information class it serves, the two memory types

The four properties

PropertyMeaning
AnchoredNotes attach to the place where they were spoken — a machine, a bench spot, a shelf
AccumulatedThe space builds up knowledge over time, from multiple people and projects
LayeredNotes carry persistence levels (ephemeral / project-lived / permanent) and audiences
NavigableThe record is structured — versioned, filterable, traversable — not a pile of pins

Encounter-class vs. episode-class

Encounter-class information has low individual value, high cumulative value, a spatial referent, and an unknown next reader — channel tools price it out, so it dies. Episode-class messages have a known recipient and bounded content — Slack is the right tool, and Spatial Memory never sees them. This carve is the paper's claim boundary: we displace nothing that works; we catch what today has no carrier at all.

Two memory types — and why only two

A memory type is defined by two questions: what is the note anchored to, and what does it outlive.

TypeAnchored toOutlivesExample
Object memorya place — a fixture or a movable's home spotany project — institutional"0.6 nozzle is in" on the printer; "calibration is stale" on the rig
Project memorya Project spanning many placesnothing — lives and dies with the project"wing joint fragile" travels from the workbench to the motor-test bench

Person is not a third type. Nothing is anchored to a person, and "person memory" outlives nothing in particular. Person — like time — is a query over the graph, not a storage type: whose trail, addressed to whom, since when. This definition is also the pre-emptive answer to "why is there no person/time/task memory?"

Zero ceremony, not zero cost

The human still authors every memory — judgment is irreducible, and every memory is a deliberate utterance: no ambient inference, no gaze or dwell logging, no telemetry (validated by a 60-scenario scoring exercise in which every inferred-event design was rejected). What the system removes is only the removable tax: recipient selection, referent description, app switching. This is also the privacy stance — not surveillance, but capture of chosen speech.

4Memory Graph paper's job: the mechanism — small enough to state in a sentence, strong enough to carry the structure claim

The whole graph in one sentence: a note is pinned at a place, signed by a person, optionally belongs to a project, and can supersede an older note. Everything the system does is five operations over that structure.

Definition
Nodes (4)Note · Place (a SLAM zone — a fixture, or a movable's home spot; "objects" are just named places) · Person · Project
Edges (5)anchored_to (note→place) · left_by (note→person) · directed_to (note→person/role) · for_project (note→project) · supersedes (note→note)
Note properties (2)audience (who should hear it) · persistence (ephemeral / project-lived / permanent)
Queries (4)notes_at(place) · for_me(person) · trail(person) · project_state(project)

SLAM-only constraint. The camera is a locator, never a recorder: no visual content, no small-object recognition. Movables get a home-spot Place, are named in the utterance ("the plugged-in Pixel"), and ride project memory across stations. This constraint is precisely why both memory types are necessary — the zone anchor covers the encounter, project memory covers everywhere else the warning must travel; only the pair covers both.

6Scenario paper's job: one week, four sub-problems dramatized as near-misses with victims — never a feature tour

Cast: Jesse (senior; drone build; also records mocap ground-truth data for a separate wearable-sensing project), Vasco (study prototype; printer-heavy), Mia (a different team entirely; mocap user study), Taeyoung (joins the drone build mid-week).

The lab

Picture a shared research lab: a 3D printer, a motion-capture rig, a soldering bench, and a shelf of shared test devices — phones, watches, headsets, laptops. Several project teams share the room, but they almost never share the clock. The morning person leaves before the afternoon person arrives. The room is the only "member" present across every shift — and it remembers nothing.

The week before — without Spatial Memory

Tuesday, 4 pm. Jesse is rushing a coarse draft print, so he swaps the printer's 0.4 mm nozzle for a 0.6. A thought crosses his mind: "I should tell whoever prints next." He doesn't. He would have to put down the work, find his phone, pick a channel, explain which printer and which nozzle — and he doesn't even know who prints next. The cost is small. It is not zero. He moves on.

Five minutes later, at the workbench, the drone's left wing joint flexes under his thumb. Fragile. "Whoever runs the motor test should brace it first." He doesn't know who that will be. He doesn't want to spam the channel. He moves on.

Wednesday: Vasco prints a fine part through the wrong nozzle, using the default profile. Two hours, ruined. Thursday: Taeyoung runs the motor test. The joint snaps.

Notice that Jesse did not forget. He ran a rational calculation: the cost of composing each message exceeded its value to him. Everyone made the locally correct decision, and the information died anyway. The room saw everything — it watched Jesse swap the nozzle, watched him flex the joint. The room cannot speak.

Monday — the same week, with Spatial Memory

Jesse swaps the nozzle again. This time, without stopping, he says one sentence into the room: "Put the 0.6 in to rush a draft — swap back to 0.4 before anything fine, the slicer profile won't warn you." That's all. No app, no recipient. The sentence is pinned to the printer.

At the workbench he says another: "Left wing joint is fragile — brace it before the motor test." The system splits this one utterance into two cards: one pinned to the fuselage's spot on the workbench — the system doesn't recognize the fuselage; it remembers where the words were spoken — and one filed to the Drone Build project, so the warning can follow the project wherever it goes. Jesse classifies nothing.

That same day, Jesse also uses Slack — to confirm Thursday's motor-test slot with Taeyoung. Known recipient, bounded message, nothing anchored to a place: Slack is the right tool, and Spatial Memory never sees it.

Tuesday — object memory works; a stranger plants a warning

Vasco walks up to print a fine part. Before he hits print, Jesse's note surfaces — at the machine, at the moment it matters. He swaps the 0.4 back in and says: "0.4 is back in, profile matches again." His sentence supersedes Jesse's. This is not a pile of sticky notes growing forever; it is a record with versions, and the place shows its current state.

Loading filament, he adds: "This PLA roll has been absorbing moisture — drying it overnight, use the grey roll." And at the device shelf he starts a test: "The plugged-in Pixel is mid-way through a 48-hour battery log — screen's off but it's running, don't touch."

Meanwhile Mia — a different team entirely — packs up the mocap rig after her study. She had to move two cameras to clear her setup. She says: "Moved two cameras packing up — calibration is stale, recalibrate before you trust your data." She has no idea who needs the rig next, or when. She doesn't need to. The sentence waits on the rig.

Wednesday — the warning crosses project lines

Jesse sets up a two-hour motion-capture session — ground-truth recording for his wearable-sensing project, nothing to do with the drone. As he approaches the rig, Mia's warning surfaces. He recalibrates. Consider what would have happened without it: a stale calibration throws no error — the software still shows the last calibration as valid — so the data would have been silently corrupted, discovered days later during processing. Two hours, gone. A single sentence from someone he has never worked with saved the session.

You might object: doesn't the lab have a #mocap channel? It does, and Mia could have posted there. But by Wednesday the message is deep in scrollback, and Jesse — standing at the rig, about to press record — is not going to re-read a channel. The message would exist; it just wouldn't be where it matters, when it matters. And a channel has a deeper problem: it is a timeline. It cannot answer "is the calibration good right now?" — has anyone recalibrated since Mia posted? On Slack, that's archaeology. On the rig, it's a state: whoever recalibrates says so, and the old warning is superseded. The next person always sees the current answer, not the history.

One more small moment: Jesse reaches for a test phone. The shelf shows Vasco's note from yesterday — the plugged-in Pixel is mid-log, don't touch — and he takes the other phone. A sleeping phone and a logging phone look exactly the same; a camera could stare at the shelf forever and see nothing. The state existed only in Vasco's head, and the system's entire job was to keep the one sentence he chose to say, at the place where it's needed.

Thursday — the newcomer

Taeyoung joins the drone build mid-stream. He walks in and asks: "Anything for me?" He gets three cards, in dependency order — not forty. Then he follows Jesse's trail through the build — workbench, printer, soldering bench — each station replaying what was left there, in order. At the workbench he lifts the fuselage, and Monday's warning fires at the moment of pickup: joint is fragile, brace it. He braces it. Last week, Thursday was the day the joint snapped. This Thursday, nothing happens at all.

Friday — the failure Spatial Memory creates, and must solve

One week, three projects, dozens of notes. If the room replayed all of them raw, Jesse would drown the moment he walked in — anchoring alone would have reproduced the spam problem, in space. What actually surfaces: expired ephemera are gone, superseded notes are collapsed, everything not addressed to him is filtered out. He hears two things, in the right order.

This is the heart of the argument. Pinning messages in space is the easy half — anyone can do it. Letting a room accumulate hundreds of records while each arriver hears only what they need — that requires structure: who said it, what it's anchored to, which project it belongs to, what supersedes what, who it's for. That structure is the Memory Graph.

Closing — why none of this is Slack

Three sentences. First: these messages have no nameable recipient at the moment of writing. Second: they point at physical things whose critical property is invisible — describing it in words loses it, and the reader can't re-ground it. Third: they are useful only at the object, at the moment of encounter — on a phone they arrive in the wrong place at the wrong time. And the camera can't save you either, because what matters is not what happened but what a person judged — a swapped nozzle looks identical on video. The judgment must be spoken by the person who holds it. Our system's entire job is to make speaking it cost nothing, and to let the room carry it to whoever needs it next, where it happened.

7System paper's job: a simple, honest implementation — the vehicle for the demo video and the studies, no more

Committed by the beats — nothing speculative (build in this order)

  • ① Zero-ceremony anchored voice capture — A1 A2 B2 B3
  • ③ Surfacing on approach to anchor — B1 C1 C2 D2  (① + ③ = MVP; alone they demo the A1→B1 thread)
  • ② LLM auto-segmentation: one utterance → object + project cards — A2
  • ④ Supersede / versioning — B1 E1, and the log-vs-state argument
  • ⑥ Relevance triage + audience filter + ordering — D1 E1
  • ⑤ Project spine + trail replay — A2 D1
  • ⑦ Persistence levels (ephemera expire) — E1

Excluded by design

  • No camera-based device-state or pickup detection — C2 works by utterance
  • No small-object visual recognition — SLAM zone/fixture anchors only; movables = home-spot anchor + named in the utterance + project memory
  • No gaze / dwell logging, no telemetry, no inferred events
  • No laser cutter in the scenario — four fixtures are enough; multiplicity was the old scenario's failure
  • Rule: every feature maps to a beat; a feature with no beat doesn't get built

Platform: Meta Quest 3 / 3S as a functional proxy for future lightweight AR glasses (shared spatial anchors, mic, passthrough). Voice → on-device/edge STT → LLM segmentation → Memory Graph → surfacing client.

8Evaluation paper's job: one chained pipeline answering three RQs, every number traceable to real in-flow speech

Goal. Study 1 tests whether people speak when the ceremony is gone (sub-problem A). Study 2 tests whether structure — not mere anchoring — makes the accumulated record usable (sub-problem D). The chain: voice notes that real participants produce in Study 1 are structured by the real pipeline and become the room that Study 2 participants walk into; the same corpus serves as the technical-eval benchmark.

Cut by design: deployment. No one wears today's headsets through real workdays. The premise that lightweight all-day glasses arrive is the paper's opening bet (industry trajectory), not the study's burden. The studies evaluate the marginal-ceremony claim — given you are already wearing the device, is speaking in place better than the channel?

Research questions

RQQuestionMeasures
RQ1 · captureDoes zero-ceremony anchored voice increase in-flow externalization of encounter-class observations, relative to current practice (phone + Slack)?RQ1a externalization rate · RQ1b flow cost (time-off-task, NASA-TLX) · RQ1c usefulness of recorded notes (blinded rating)

The chain

Study 1 utterances (real, recorded + located) → real LLM segmentation, run offline (RQ3 is measured here, on this corpus) → a curated frozen seed set: one representative real note per trap, plus experimenter-authored fillers to complete the week (other projects, a cross-team rig note) → identical pre-seeded rooms for every Study 2 participant. No live relay — if each arriver consumed a different leaver's notes, no two participants would face the same room and conditions would not compare. Collect first, curate once, freeze. Reported honestly: "seed notes were drawn from Study 1 participants' recordings, supplemented by researcher-authored notes to constitute a full week."

Study 1 — the Leaver (RQ1) · N ≈ 10 · ~40 min

Cover story: "You're the morning shift; someone you don't know takes over this station this afternoon. Do the job; handle anything you notice however you normally would." Recording is never mentioned — it stays optional and costly, exactly like life. Both conditions wear the headset (passthrough), so device burden is constant and the only variable is the capture channel: SM (speak in place; wizarded — audio + location genuinely recorded, no live pipeline) vs. Slack (phone in pocket, lab channels available). Within-subject, two matched task variants, counterbalanced.

Task (variant A — "sensor-mount kit," ~30 min, mild time pressure): collect a draft print → assemble the bracket → solder a connector → stage a logging phone for a 48-hour test. Variant B mirrors it (enclosure kit). Six planted encounters per variant — three caused by the participant, three discovered:

Study 2 — the Arriver (RQ2) · N = 12–16 · ~50 min · Quest 3

Cover story: "You're the afternoon shift, new to this project. Get up to speed, then complete three jobs." Conditions, within-subject on two matched rooms ("Week A" / "Week B," counterbalanced): SM-full (graph on — triage, supersession, audience filter, trail) vs. anchored-only (same notes, raw in place: stale nozzle note hangs beside the new one, no filter, no expiry, no triage — the ablation). ~35–40 notes per room, including superseded pairs and notes addressed to others, so structure has real work to do. Slack is not a Study 2 condition: it was the baseline on the capture side; Study 2 isolates the structure claim, and two conditions keep matched-room preparation feasible.

9Discussion paper's job: name the portable concepts, be honest about limits — the ammunition list

ThreadThe argument to make
Log vs. stateGeneralize the channel critique: communication tools store time-ordered logs, but physical work needs current state at a place; supersession is the primitive that converts one into the other. Portable beyond AR.
Camera as locatorThe privacy stance as a design position: today's 6DoF needs cameras, but the system uses them only for pose — no content, no recognition, no inference. When camera-less positioning matures, the paradigm transfers unchanged.
