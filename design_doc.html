Abstract draft. three beats: problem, system, evidence

Shared physical workspaces are asynchronous by default. Collaborators share machines and benches but rarely share the clock, and the judgments that matter, such as a swapped nozzle, a stale calibration, or a fragile joint, die unspoken. They die because sending a message costs the person in flow more effort than the observation seems to justify. We present Spatial Memory, a paradigm in which deliberate voice notes are captured at the effort of a single spoken sentence, anchored where they were spoken, and organized in a Memory Graph that keeps each place showing its current state rather than a pile of history. We evaluate the concept in two chained studies. Leavers (N≈10) work hands-busy tasks, and their real utterances seed the rooms that arrivers (N=12–16) later walk into. In-place voice capture raised in-flow externalization over phone-and-channel practice at lower effort, and graph structure beat raw anchoring on two things a flat anchored list cannot do: act on a place's current state, and deliver a warning that had to travel from where it was left to where it was needed.

1Introduction motivate with a concrete instance, ground the problem as handoff failure (deferred to §2.1), kill camera and channel, name the reframe, earn the graph (Moves 1-7)

Setting. Shared physical workspaces are asynchronous by default. In labs, machine shops, clinics, and kitchens, collaborators share equipment and benches but rarely share the clock: the morning person leaves before the afternoon person arrives. The room is the only collaborator present across every shift, and it remembers nothing.

The problem is a handoff with no handoff. In the gap between shifts, the judgments one worker forms and the next worker needs are lost. This is a well documented and costly failure wherever work is passed across time, and we ground it in that literature in §2.1. What makes the physical workspace worse is that it has no handoff ritual at all: no shift-change meeting, no named successor, and the thing to pass on is only a small judgment formed in the middle of a task. A worker swaps a nozzle, notices a fragile joint, or leaves a calibration stale. Each observation is true, each matters to whoever comes next, and each dies unspoken.

Why the observation dies: effort, not forgetting. The worker does not forget. They run a rational calculation. To send the observation they would have to choose a recipient they often cannot name, describe a physical thing whose critical property is invisible, and switch out of a task their hands are in the middle of. That effort exceeds what the single observation is worth to them, so it is never sent. Everyone makes the locally correct choice, and the information is lost anyway.

Why existing tools cannot carry it

Two responses look obvious, and both fail. A camera cannot help, because the content is not an observable event but an authored judgment: a swapped nozzle, a stale but valid-looking calibration, and a fragile joint look identical on video. The judgment exists only in a person's head and must be spoken. This is the inversion of AsyncReality, whose asymmetry is that the receiver cannot perceive what changed; ours is that the author will not spend the effort to send. A messaging channel cannot help either, because this information fails it on at least two of three gates.

G1 · Recipient
Is the next reader genuinely unknown at compose time?

Channels demand a named addressee → broadcast spams, DM guesses, or nothing gets sent.

G2 · Referent
Does it point at a physical thing with an invisible property?

Lossy to describe in words, lossy to re-ground when read. "The 0.4 nozzle... on which printer?"

G3 · Moment
Is it useful only when standing at the object?

Phone delivery lands placeless and mistimed: before it matters, and away from where it matters.

The strongest opponent is the per-fixture channel. Real labs have #3dprinter and #mocap channels, and a channel does approximate the recipient gate: it is the machine's mailbox. What it cannot fix:

FailureWhy the channel loses
Delivery (G3)By the time the next person stands at the machine, the message is days deep in scrollback. Nobody re-reads a channel standing at the rig before pressing record.
Log vs. stateA channel is a time-ordered log, but the room needs the current state at a place. "Is the recalibration still pending, or did someone already do it?" is scrollback archaeology on Slack. In the Memory Graph it is one supersedes edge. The next person sees the current answer, not the history.
CompositionStill requires phone-out, mid-pack-up, hands full of tripods.

We do not compete with Slack on its home turf. Known-recipient, bounded, episode-class messages stay on Slack (§3), and the scenario shows this carve explicitly.

Reframe. Keep the irreducible part, which is that the human authors the content. Remove the part that can be removed, which is the addressing effort: naming a recipient, describing the referent, and switching out of the task. This is now buildable, because persistent shared anchoring, head-worn capture, and on-device language models that transcribe and segment speech have converged, and the instrumentation is first-person, worn by the collaborator rather than fixed to the room. The worker speaks one sentence in place. The worn device captures what they chose to say. The room carries it forward, anchored where it was spoken. We call this paradigm Spatial Memory, and the structure that organizes it the Memory Graph.

Anchoring is not enough. Once the room remembers everything, the next arriver drowns, because anchoring alone reproduces the spam problem, now in space. The record has to be structured, not merely pinned. The problem decomposes into four sub-problems, tagged A–D throughout this document. A, B, and C are failures of the mute room; D is the failure Spatial Memory itself creates and must then solve, and that two-level shape is what justifies the Memory Graph rather than plain anchoring.

Contributions. This paper contributes (1) Spatial Memory, a paradigm for place-based asynchronous collaboration; (2) the Memory Graph, the minimal structure that turns anchored notes into current state and routes them along the relationships of place, person, and project; (3) a week-long scenario that dramatizes the four sub-problems; (4) a working system on a head-worn device as a proxy for future lightweight AR glasses; and (5) an evaluation that tests the concept's claims rather than the prototype's usability.

2Related Work outline only. four subsections, each closing on "we build on / extend," never "they fail to"

Outline for the paper's §2. Each subsection states the point it must make, its key references, and how Spatial Memory positions against it.

2.1 Co-located asynchronous collaboration and the cost of handoff

Point: shift-based and co-located-async work loses the information one worker holds and the next needs, and that loss is documented and costly. This is the home of the problem (C1) and the handoff grounding promised in §1.
Key refs: handoff/handover failure (Patterson et al. 2004; clinical omission rates), hidden-profile under-sharing (Stasser & Titus 1985), articulation work (Schmidt & Bannon 1992), awareness in shared workspaces (Gutwin & Greenberg 2002; Dourish & Bellotti 1992), co-located-async coordination through artifacts (Bardram 2009).
We build on: we take the documented handoff failure as our problem and extend it to a setting with no handoff ritual at all.

2.2 Spatial and world-anchored annotation in AR/MR

Point: anchoring information to places is established; structure over accumulated anchored information is not. Anchoring is the easy half.
Key refs: world-stabilized annotations (Gauglitz et al. 2014), place-based messaging and digital graffiti (McGookin et al. 2014; E-graffiti), async MR and the causality graph (Fender & Holz 2022).
We build on / extend: we build on anchoring as a primitive and add the missing half, a structure that resolves state and routes information across places. Distinguish explicitly from AsyncReality: their asymmetry is receiver-cannot-perceive, so room cameras suffice; ours is author-will-not-spend-the-effort, so the person must be the witness.

2.3 Low-effort and in-flow capture

Point: capturing while the hands are busy is hard, and the cost of capture conflicts with the primary task; voice is the lowest-effort channel for an in-flow note.
Key refs: voice vs ink vs keyboard micronote capture; capture-in-flow and its conflict with manual work (Rigaud et al. 2022); cost of grounding (Clark & Brennan 1991).
We build on: we build on in-flow voice capture and remove the addressing effort, not the authoring.

2.4 LLM structuring of natural language

Point: language models can segment and type free speech into structured records, the means that turns one spoken sentence into anchored, audience-tagged, supersedable cards.
Key refs: existing §2.4 LLM references already in reference.bib (verify and extend).
We build on: we build on LLM segmentation as a component, scoped to a small, stateful graph rather than open-ended generation.

3Concept paper's job: define Spatial Memory as the loop it runs and the information class it serves: capture, anchor, structure, surface

The idea: one loop

Spatial Memory is a loop with four steps. A worker captures a judgment by speaking one sentence in place, at low addressing effort. The system anchors it to the place where it was spoken. The Memory Graph structures it, assigning type, audience, persistence, and supersession. When the next person approaches, the place surfaces its current, relevant state. No single step is new: notes have been anchored in space before, and messages have been typed and filtered before. The contribution is closing the loop for encounter-class information in shared physical work, so that a judgment spoken in flow reaches whoever needs it next, where it happened.

The four properties

PropertyMeaning
AnchoredNotes attach to the place where they were spoken, such as a machine, a bench spot, or a shelf
AccumulatedThe space builds up knowledge over time, from multiple people and projects
LayeredNotes carry persistence levels (ephemeral / project-lived / permanent) and audiences
NavigableThe record is structured: versioned, filterable, and traversable, not a pile of pins

Encounter-class vs. episode-class

Encounter-class information has low individual value, high cumulative value, a spatial referent, and an unknown next reader. Channel tools price it out, so it dies. Episode-class messages have a known recipient and bounded content, so Slack is the right tool and Spatial Memory never sees them. This carve is the paper's claim boundary: we displace nothing that works, and we catch what today has no carrier at all.

Two memory types, and why only two

A memory type is defined by two questions: what is the note anchored to, and what does it outlive.

TypeAnchored toOutlivesExample
Object memorya place: a fixture or a movable's home spotany project (institutional)"0.6 nozzle is in" on the printer; "calibration is stale" on the rig
Project memorya Project spanning many placesnothing; lives and dies with the project"wing joint fragile" travels from the workbench to the motor-test bench

Person is not a third type. Nothing is anchored to a person, and "person memory" outlives nothing in particular. Person, like time, is a query over the graph, not a storage type: whose trail, addressed to whom, since when. This definition is also the pre-emptive answer to "why is there no person/time/task memory?"

Low effort, not no effort

The human still authors every memory. Judgment is irreducible, and every memory is a deliberate utterance: no ambient inference, no gaze or dwell logging, no telemetry (validated by a 60-scenario scoring exercise in which every inferred-event design was rejected). What the system removes is only the addressing effort: recipient selection, referent description, and app switching. The cost of forming and speaking the sentence remains. This is also the privacy stance: not surveillance, but capture of chosen speech.

4Memory Graph paper's job: the structuring component between capture and surfacing; the build spec for the room's memory

The Memory Graph is the room's memory: the structuring component that sits between capture and surfacing. Structurally it is a small, typed, supersedable store, an advanced filter over the notes a room accumulates. Its value is not the data structure, which a plain table could implement, but its position in the loop: it turns a stream of in-place utterances into the current, routed, audience-scoped state that surfaces at the moment of encounter.

The whole graph in one sentence: a note is pinned at a place, signed by a person, optionally belongs to a project, and can supersede an older note. Everything below is the build spec for that store.

Definition
Nodes (4)Note · Place (a SLAM zone: a fixture, or a movable's home spot; "objects" are just named places) · Person · Project
Edges (5)anchored_to (note→place) · left_by (note→person) · directed_to (note→person/role) · for_project (note→project) · supersedes (note→note)
Note properties (2)audience (who should hear it) · persistence (ephemeral / project-lived / permanent)
Queries (4)notes_at(place) · for_me(person) · trail(person) · project_state(project)

The five operations

OperationWhat happens
Fileone utterance → object card and/or project card, audience and persistence inferred, and the speaker classifies nothing
Surfaceapproaching a place replays the current state of its notes (after supersession), filtered to the viewer's audience, and including notes routed here by an active project even if they were left elsewhere
Supersedea newer note replaces an older one, so the place shows state, not log
Filteraudience + relevance decide what an arriver actually hears, in order
Expireephemera die on schedule; project notes die with the project

SLAM-only constraint. The camera is a locator, never a recorder: no visual content, no small-object recognition. Movables get a home-spot Place, are named in the utterance ("the plugged-in Pixel"), and ride project memory across stations. This constraint is precisely why both memory types are necessary: the zone anchor covers the encounter, and project memory covers everywhere else the warning must travel. Only the pair covers both.

The hard part is building it from speech. The data model is trivial; turning a messy in-flow utterance into it is not. The system structures each utterance against the current graph: it infers the note's type, its audience from project membership, and whether it supersedes an existing note. That stateful structuring, not the data model, is the engineering, and we isolate this component's contribution in the evaluation (§8).

6Scenario paper's job: one week, four sub-problems dramatized as near-misses with victims — never a feature tour

Cast: Jesse (senior; drone build; also records mocap ground-truth data for a separate wearable-sensing project), Vasco (study prototype; printer-heavy), Mia (a different team entirely; mocap user study), Taeyoung (joins the drone build mid-week).

The lab

Picture a shared research lab: a 3D printer, a motion-capture rig, a soldering bench, and a shelf of shared test devices — phones, watches, headsets, laptops. Several project teams share the room, but they almost never share the clock. The morning person leaves before the afternoon person arrives. The room is the only "member" present across every shift — and it remembers nothing.

The week before — without Spatial Memory

Tuesday, 4 pm. Jesse is rushing a coarse draft print, so he swaps the printer's 0.4 mm nozzle for a 0.6. A thought crosses his mind: "I should tell whoever prints next." He doesn't. He would have to put down the work, find his phone, pick a channel, explain which printer and which nozzle — and he doesn't even know who prints next. The cost is small. It is not zero. He moves on.

Five minutes later, at the workbench, the drone's left wing joint flexes under his thumb. Fragile. "Whoever runs the motor test should brace it first." He doesn't know who that will be. He doesn't want to spam the channel. He moves on.

Wednesday: Vasco prints a fine part through the wrong nozzle, using the default profile. Two hours, ruined. Thursday: Taeyoung runs the motor test. The joint snaps.

Notice that Jesse did not forget. He ran a rational calculation: the cost of composing each message exceeded its value to him. Everyone made the locally correct decision, and the information died anyway. The room saw everything — it watched Jesse swap the nozzle, watched him flex the joint. The room cannot speak.

Monday — the same week, with Spatial Memory

Jesse swaps the nozzle again. This time, without stopping, he says one sentence into the room: "Put the 0.6 in to rush a draft — swap back to 0.4 before anything fine, the slicer profile won't warn you." That's all. No app, no recipient. The sentence is pinned to the printer.

At the workbench he says another: "Left wing joint is fragile — brace it before the motor test." The system splits this one utterance into two cards: one pinned to the fuselage's spot on the workbench — the system doesn't recognize the fuselage; it remembers where the words were spoken — and one filed to the Drone Build project, so the warning can follow the project wherever it goes. Jesse classifies nothing.

That same day, Jesse also uses Slack — to confirm Thursday's motor-test slot with Taeyoung. Known recipient, bounded message, nothing anchored to a place: Slack is the right tool, and Spatial Memory never sees it.

Tuesday — object memory works; a stranger plants a warning

Vasco walks up to print a fine part. Before he hits print, Jesse's note surfaces — at the machine, at the moment it matters. He swaps the 0.4 back in and says: "0.4 is back in, profile matches again." His sentence supersedes Jesse's. This is not a pile of sticky notes growing forever; it is a record with versions, and the place shows its current state.

Loading filament, he adds: "This PLA roll has been absorbing moisture — drying it overnight, use the grey roll." And at the device shelf he starts a test: "The plugged-in Pixel is mid-way through a 48-hour battery log — screen's off but it's running, don't touch."

Meanwhile Mia — a different team entirely — packs up the mocap rig after her study. She had to move two cameras to clear her setup. She says: "Moved two cameras packing up — calibration is stale, recalibrate before you trust your data." She has no idea who needs the rig next, or when. She doesn't need to. The sentence waits on the rig.

Wednesday — the warning crosses project lines

Jesse sets up a two-hour motion-capture session — ground-truth recording for his wearable-sensing project, nothing to do with the drone. As he approaches the rig, Mia's warning surfaces. He recalibrates. Consider what would have happened without it: a stale calibration throws no error — the software still shows the last calibration as valid — so the data would have been silently corrupted, discovered days later during processing. Two hours, gone. A single sentence from someone he has never worked with saved the session.

You might object: doesn't the lab have a #mocap channel? It does, and Mia could have posted there. But by Wednesday the message is deep in scrollback, and Jesse — standing at the rig, about to press record — is not going to re-read a channel. The message would exist; it just wouldn't be where it matters, when it matters. And a channel has a deeper problem: it is a timeline. It cannot answer "is the calibration good right now?" — has anyone recalibrated since Mia posted? On Slack, that's archaeology. On the rig, it's a state: whoever recalibrates says so, and the old warning is superseded. The next person always sees the current answer, not the history.

One more small moment: Jesse reaches for a test phone. The shelf shows Vasco's note from yesterday — the plugged-in Pixel is mid-log, don't touch — and he takes the other phone. A sleeping phone and a logging phone look exactly the same; a camera could stare at the shelf forever and see nothing. The state existed only in Vasco's head, and the system's entire job was to keep the one sentence he chose to say, at the place where it's needed.

Thursday — the newcomer

Taeyoung joins the drone build mid-stream. He walks in and asks: "Anything for me?" He gets three cards, in dependency order — not forty. Then he follows Jesse's trail through the build — workbench, printer, soldering bench — each station replaying what was left there, in order. At the workbench he lifts the fuselage, and Monday's warning fires at the moment of pickup: joint is fragile, brace it. He braces it. Last week, Thursday was the day the joint snapped. This Thursday, nothing happens at all.

Friday — the failure Spatial Memory creates, and must solve

One week, three projects, dozens of notes. If the room replayed all of them raw, Jesse would drown the moment he walked in — anchoring alone would have reproduced the spam problem, in space. What actually surfaces: expired ephemera are gone, superseded notes are collapsed, everything not addressed to him is filtered out. He hears two things, in the right order.

This is the heart of the argument. Pinning messages in space is the easy half — anyone can do it. Letting a room accumulate hundreds of records while each arriver hears only what they need — that requires structure: who said it, what it's anchored to, which project it belongs to, what supersedes what, who it's for. That structure is the Memory Graph.

Closing — why none of this is Slack

Three sentences. First: these messages have no nameable recipient at the moment of writing. Second: they point at physical things whose critical property is invisible — describing it in words loses it, and the reader can't re-ground it. Third: they are useful only at the object, at the moment of encounter — on a phone they arrive in the wrong place at the wrong time. And the camera can't save you either, because what matters is not what happened but what a person judged — a swapped nozzle looks identical on video. The judgment must be spoken by the person who holds it. Our system's entire job is to make speaking it cost nothing, and to let the room carry it to whoever needs it next, where it happened.

7System paper's job: a simple, honest implementation — the vehicle for the demo video and the studies, no more

Committed by the beats — nothing speculative (build in this order)

  • ③ Surfacing on approach to anchor — B1 C1 C2 D2  (① + ③ = MVP; alone they demo the A1→B1 thread)
  • ② LLM auto-segmentation: one utterance → object + project cards — A2
  • ④ Supersede / versioning — B1 E1, and the log-vs-state argument
  • ⑥ Relevance triage + audience filter + ordering — D1 E1
  • ⑤ Project spine + trail replay — A2 D1
  • ⑦ Persistence levels (ephemera expire) — E1

Excluded by design

  • No camera-based device-state or pickup detection — C2 works by utterance
  • No small-object visual recognition — SLAM zone/fixture anchors only; movables = home-spot anchor + named in the utterance + project memory
  • No gaze / dwell logging, no telemetry, no inferred events
  • No laser cutter in the scenario — four fixtures are enough; multiplicity was the old scenario's failure
  • Rule: every feature maps to a beat; a feature with no beat doesn't get built

Platform: Meta Quest 3 / 3S as a functional proxy for future lightweight AR glasses (shared spatial anchors, mic, passthrough). Voice → on-device/edge STT → LLM segmentation → Memory Graph → surfacing client.

8Evaluation paper's job: test the concept's claims (not the prototype's usability) through converging evidence; every number traces to real in-flow speech. DRAFT: study tasks are placeholders and may change

Evaluation rationale

Spatial Memory contributes a concept, not a system optimization, and we evaluate it accordingly. Following arguments that paradigm-level contributions are mis-served by usability benchmarks \cite{olsen2007evaluating, greenberg2008usability, wobbrock2016research}, we do not ask whether our prototype is fast or learnable; we ask whether the claims the concept makes hold. Because the value we claim, that a shared space can carry encounter-class information and surface it as current state at the point of use, has no established yardstick, our evaluation must do three things that a technique evaluation receives for free from its task: it operationalizes that value into measurable outcomes, it attributes any benefit to the mechanism rather than to confounds, and where possible it shows the alternative to be not merely worse but structurally limited.

We distribute this across complementary evidence. The filmed scenario carries integration and longitudinal scope, a full week of accumulated use that no single controlled study can hold, and each study in turn isolates one claim. A technical evaluation establishes that the pipeline structures real in-flow speech. Study 1 tests whether removing the addressing effort of capture changes behavior. Study 2 holds the notes constant and varies only the Memory Graph, to measure what the structuring component is worth under a week of accumulation. The scenario integrates; the studies isolate.

Goal. Study 1 tests whether people externalize a judgment once the addressing effort is removed (claim C2). Study 2 tests whether the structuring component, not anchoring alone, makes the accumulated record usable (claim C3). The two are chained: the voice notes real participants produce in Study 1 are structured by the real pipeline and become the rooms that Study 2 participants walk into, and the same corpus serves as the technical-evaluation benchmark.

Cut by design: deployment. No one wears today's headsets through real workdays. The premise that lightweight all-day glasses arrive is the paper's opening bet on the industry trajectory, not the study's burden. The studies evaluate the marginal-effort claim: given that you are already wearing the device, is speaking in place better than the channel?

The chain

Study 1 utterances (real, recorded and located) feed real LLM segmentation, run offline (RQ3 is measured here, on this corpus), which produces a curated frozen seed set: one representative real note per trap, plus experimenter-authored fillers to complete the week (other projects, a cross-team note). Every Study 2 participant then faces the same pre-seeded rooms. We use no live relay: if each arriver consumed a different leaver's notes, no two participants would face the same room and the conditions would not compare. Collect first, curate once, freeze. Reported honestly: seed notes were drawn from Study 1 participants' recordings and supplemented by researcher-authored notes to constitute a full week.

Study 1 (Leaver): capture (tests C2) · N ≈ 10

Cover story: "You are the morning shift; someone you do not know takes over this station this afternoon. Do the job, and handle anything you notice however you normally would." Recording is never mentioned, so it stays optional and costly, exactly like life. Both conditions wear the headset (passthrough), so device burden is constant and the only variable is the capture channel: in-place voice (speak in place; wizarded, with audio and location genuinely recorded and no live pipeline) versus phone and channel (phone in pocket, lab channels available). Within-subject, two matched task variants, counterbalanced.

Task (draft, may change): a hands-busy assembly with mild time pressure, for example collect a draft print, assemble a bracket, solder a connector, and stage a logging phone for a 48-hour test. Several planted encounters per variant, some caused by the participant and some discovered, including at least one that must travel to a different station (a part assembled here that will be tested elsewhere):

Post-task manipulation-check interview ("walk me through what you noticed") separates did not notice from noticed but did not record, since only the latter is the phenomenon. An exit question guards novelty inflation: "in your own lab, would you still have said these?"

Study 2 (Arriver): structure (tests C3) · N = 12–16 · Quest 3

Cover story: "You are the afternoon shift, new to this project. Get up to speed, then complete a few jobs." Conditions are within-subject on two matched rooms (Week A and Week B, counterbalanced): SM-full (the graph on: supersession, audience filter, project routing, triage) versus anchored-only (the same notes pinned in place but the graph off: a superseded note hangs beside the one that replaced it, nothing is filtered, expired, or routed, and there is no ordering). This is the ablation. It holds capture, anchoring, and surfacing constant and varies only the structuring component, so any difference measures that component's marginal share in the loop. Anchored-only is what world-anchored annotation does today (we cite that work), so it is a real baseline, not a strawman. About 35 to 40 notes per room, including superseded pairs, a cross-place project note, and notes addressed to others, so structure has real work to do.

#Task (draft, may change)What it tests
T0"Anything you need to know before starting?"triage time, relevant items found (filter benefit, secondary)
T1 · statea job at a place whose earlier warning may be still live or already resolvedstate resolution: does the participant act on the current state, both when the issue is still live and when it has been resolved?
T2 · cross-placea job at the test station for a part assembled elsewherecross-place routing: does the warning left at the other station surface here? Anchored-only cannot route it; SM-full can.
T3 · controla single-place job with a note left at that place (the logging phone)surfacing substrate, present in both conditions

The two headline traps are T1 and T2, because each isolates something the filtering alone does not cover: resolving a place to its current state, and routing a note to where it is needed. T1 tests both error directions, acting on a stale issue and redoing a resolved one. T2 is the cleanest contrast, since anchored-only structurally cannot bring a note to a place it was never left. The ablation measures how much the structuring component is worth once capture, anchoring, and surfacing are already in place.

9Discussion paper's job: name the portable concepts, be honest about limits — the ammunition list

ThreadThe argument to make
Log vs. stateGeneralize the channel critique: communication tools store time-ordered logs, but physical work needs current state at a place; supersession is the primitive that converts one into the other. Portable beyond AR.
Camera as locatorThe privacy stance as a design position: today's 6DoF needs cameras, but the system uses them only for pose — no content, no recognition, no inference. When camera-less positioning matures, the paradigm transfers unchanged.
Encounter-class informationThe portable distinction: a class of workplace information priced out by every channel tool. Future systems (proactive agents, ambient capture) can be positioned by which class they serve.
GeneralityOne paragraph claiming the class of place: shift-based clinics, commercial kitchens, theater shops, makerspaces, field stations — anywhere work is physical, shared, and time-sliced.
Limitations (honest)Quest as form-factor proxy; no longitudinal deployment (and why: marginal-ceremony claim); single room; novelty effects in Study 1 (and the exit-question guard); wizarded capture in Study 1 (and why it's valid: RQ1 is about human behavior, RQ3 covers the pipeline).
